Project Overview

This project applies a progressive four-stage NLP pipeline to the Yelp Academic Dataset (50,000 reviews). Each stage builds on the previous, exploring how performance and capability evolve as methods advance from traditional feature engineering to a fully agentic retrieval-augmented system.

Stage 1 — Baseline Comparison

Three modeling paradigms are compared on the 5-class star rating prediction task (test set).

ModelAccuracyMacro F1
TF-IDF + Logistic Regression56.92%56.56%
Sentence-BERT + Logistic Regression53.25%52.89%
BERT (fine-tuned)62.50%62.50%

Why does fine-tuned BERT win? End-to-end training jointly optimises the text representation and the classification head for the exact task, allowing every layer to specialise. Frozen representations are fixed at inference time, so any mismatch between the pre-training objective and star-rating classification cannot be corrected.

Why does Sentence-BERT underperform TF-IDF? Sentence-BERT embeddings are optimised for semantic similarity between sentence pairs, not for single-sentence classification. Star ratings are strongly driven by specific sentiment vocabulary ("terrible", "amazing", "overpriced") — patterns that TF-IDF's word-frequency statistics capture directly, whereas Sentence-BERT compresses them into a dense vector designed for a different purpose.

Off-by-1 Accuracy explained: For star-rating prediction, a prediction is counted as "off-by-1 correct" if it falls within ±1 star of the true label (e.g. predicting 4★ when the true label is 5★ or 3★). This metric captures near-miss predictions that are practically acceptable — a user who rates a restaurant 4★ and a model that predicts 3★ are expressing nearly the same sentiment. The consistently high Off-by-1 Accuracy (≥97% for all models) reflects the inherent difficulty of distinguishing adjacent star ratings, where the linguistic signals in the review text are often ambiguous.

Why RoBERTa over BERT and DistilBERT? RoBERTa removes the Next Sentence Prediction task from pre-training, uses dynamic masking, and trains on significantly more data. This produces richer token representations that transfer more effectively to downstream tasks. DistilBERT, while faster, loses expressive capacity through knowledge distillation.

Why LR=1e-5 and batch=4? A smaller learning rate updates the pre-trained weights more gently, reducing catastrophic forgetting of linguistic knowledge. A small batch size introduces noisier gradients that act as implicit regularisation — beneficial when the fine-tuning dataset is moderately sized and overfitting is a risk.

Why is 5-class so much harder than binary? Binary classification separates clearly negative (1–2★) from clearly positive (4–5★) reviews, where sentiment signals are strong. 5-class requires distinguishing adjacent ratings (e.g. 3★ vs 4★), which often share similar vocabulary and tone. The high Off-by-1 Accuracy (≥97%) confirms the model is directionally correct — the bottleneck is the inherent ambiguity between neighbouring star levels.

Best configuration (5-class): RoBERTa-base · LR=1e-5 · batch=4 · epochs=4. This checkpoint is reused as the review classifier tool in Stage 4.

DeepSeek Prompt Engineering — Tier 2 Improvement

DeepSeek-R1 requires an intermediate prompting step (Tier 2) that Qwen skips entirely. Zero-shot performance was severely limited by an insufficient token budget and the absence of anchor examples. The following changes raised 5-class accuracy from 55.60% to 61.56%:

SettingBaseline PromptImproved Prompt
max_tokens128512
Few-shot examplesNone (zero-shot)5 examples (one per star level)
Reasoning instructionUnrestricted"Reason briefly (2–3 sentences)"
Valid prediction rate65.4%~99%
5-class accuracy55.60%61.56%

Baseline prompt (max_tokens=128, zero-shot):

You are a Yelp review rating classifier.
Analyze the review carefully.
The label must be one integer from 1 to 5.
You may reason first, but your LAST line must be exactly:
Final rating: X
where X is one of 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5.

Review: {text}

Improved prompt (max_tokens=512, 5-shot examples, brief reasoning):

You are a Yelp review rating classifier.
Reason briefly (2-3 sentences), then output your final answer.
Your LAST line must be exactly: Final rating: X  (X ∈ {1,2,3,4,5})

Here are 5 examples:
Review: "Absolutely terrible experience..." → Final rating: 1
Review: "Below average, won't return..."   → Final rating: 2
Review: "Decent, nothing special..."        → Final rating: 3
Review: "Really enjoyed it, will be back!" → Final rating: 4
Review: "Outstanding! Best in the city!"   → Final rating: 5

Review: {text}

Why did each change improve accuracy?

  1. max_tokens 128 → 512: DeepSeek-R1's chain-of-thought alone typically uses 100–300 tokens. With only 128 tokens, ~35% of responses were truncated before the "Final rating:" line ever appeared, causing parse failures. Raising the budget to 512 directly recovered those samples.
  2. 5-shot examples (one per star level): Zero-shot prompts leave the model uncertain about how each star level maps to review tone. Providing one calibrated example per level anchors the scale and forces consistent output format, reducing both format errors and label bias.
  3. "Reason briefly (2–3 sentences)": Without a length constraint, the reasoning chain can still grow long enough to exhaust 512 tokens before the final answer. Capping reasoning to 2–3 sentences ensures the label always appears within budget.

Why is Qwen's zero-shot already near fine-tune level? Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct was pre-trained on a large corpus containing review-like text and instruction-following data. It already understands the relationship between review sentiment and star ratings without task-specific training. The marginal gain from LoRA fine-tuning (+0.39% on binary) reflects task saturation — the model's capability ceiling for this task, not a limitation of fine-tuning.

Why did DeepSeek fail at zero-shot? DeepSeek-R1 is a reasoning model that generates an extended chain-of-thought before producing its final answer. With an insufficient max_tokens budget, the reasoning chain was cut off mid-way, and the model never reached a final label. This is a prompt engineering failure, not a capability failure — after increasing the token budget and adding few-shot examples, 5-class accuracy jumped from 55.6% to 61.6%.

Why does LoRA fine-tuning work effectively? LoRA freezes the base model weights and learns only small low-rank update matrices, preserving pre-trained language knowledge while adapting the output format. For strong base models, LoRA primarily achieves format alignment (e.g. outputting a single digit instead of a sentence) rather than fundamental capability improvement, which is why the accuracy gains are modest but consistent.

Why use thinking-chain data for DeepSeek fine-tuning? Directly fine-tuning DeepSeek on (input → label) pairs would suppress its chain-of-thought structure, degrading its reasoning ability. By generating thinking chains from correct predictions first and training on (input → <think>...</think> + label), the model retains its reasoning capability while learning the correct output format.

RAG Baseline (6.90/10): Fixed tool sequencing provides stable, well-grounded answers with rich evidence citation. Hallucination rate of only 5% demonstrates the effectiveness of retrieval augmentation over direct generation.

Full Agent (4.75/10): Scores highest on correctness for complex question types (Aspect Analysis, Cross-Business Pattern) where autonomous tool selection adds value. However, 7B model instability under ReAct prompting leads to 25% hallucination and poor efficiency.

Direct LLM (1.95/10): Cannot access review data, resulting in generic responses with 100% hallucination rate. Confirms that retrieval augmentation is essential for business-specific QA.
